Pouring concrete is only the first step. The chemical reaction that gives concrete its strength—called **hydration**—requires water and time. Understanding the curing timeline is critical to prevent premature loading, which can cause permanent cracking and structural failure.

## The Concrete Strength Curve

Concrete does not dry; it cures. Curing is an ongoing chemical reaction. While concrete continues to gain strength slowly for years, standard design mixes are engineered to reach their target compressive strength (measured in PSI) at 28 days.

Here is the typical strength gain timeline for standard concrete:

* **24 to 48 Hours**: Initial set. Safe for foot traffic only. Do not place heavy items or machinery.
* **7 Days**: Reaches approximately **70%** of target strength. Safe for light vehicles, passenger cars, and removing formwork.
* **28 Days**: Reaches **100%** of design strength (typically 3,000 to 4,000 PSI). Safe for heavy equipment, RV parking, and structural framing.

## Why Wet Curing is Necessary

To reach its full strength potential, concrete must retain its moisture during the first few days of curing. If the water evaporates too quickly (due to wind, sun, or heat), the hydration reaction stops, leaving the concrete weak and prone to dusting and cracking.

### Recommended Curing Methods

1. **Sprinkling/Mist Curing**: Run a lawn sprinkler or hose mist over the slab 3–5 times a day for the first 3 days.
2. **Ponding**: Build soil dikes around the slab edges and flood the surface with 1 inch of water (ideal for flat slabs).
3. **Moisture-Retaining Covers**: Cover the slab with burlap, canvas, or plastic sheeting, wetting the burlap regularly to trap moisture.
4. **Curing Compounds**: Apply a liquid membrane-forming compound immediately after finishing to seal in the hydration water.
