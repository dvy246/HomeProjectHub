Construction projects require converting between different units of measurement constantly. A lumber estimate in board feet needs to become a weight for shipping. A concrete pour in cubic feet needs to become cubic yards for ordering. This guide covers the essential conversion formulas every builder needs.

## Area Conversions

### Square Footage Basics

Square footage is the foundation of most construction estimates:

| Shape | Formula | Example |
|-------|---------|---------|
| Rectangle | Length × Width | 10×12 room = 120 sq ft |
| Circle | π × r² | 10-ft circle = 78.5 sq ft |
| Triangle | ½ × Base × Height | 10×8 triangle = 40 sq ft |
| L-shape | Divide into rectangles | Sum all sub-areas |

### Square Feet to Square Yards

Many flooring and landscaping materials are sold by the square yard:

```
Square Yards = Square Feet ÷ 9
```

**Example:** A 12×12 room is 144 sq ft = 16 sq yd.

### Square Feet to Squares (Roofing)

Roofing materials are sold in "squares" of 100 sq ft:

```
Squares = Square Feet ÷ 100
```

**Example:** 2,400 sq ft roof = 24 squares.

## Volume Conversions

### Cubic Feet to Cubic Yards

Concrete, gravel, and mulch are sold by the cubic yard:

```
Cubic Yards = Cubic Feet ÷ 27
```

The number 27 comes from 3 feet × 3 feet × 3 feet = 27 cubic feet per cubic yard.

### Cubic Yards from Linear Dimensions

For most construction calculations:

```
Cubic Yards = Length (ft) × Width (ft) × Depth (in) ÷ 324
```

Where 324 = 27 (cu ft/yard) × 12 (in/ft).

### Quick Reference: Square Feet to Cubic Yards

| Depth | 1 cu yd covers | 100 sq ft needs |
|-------|---------------|----------------|
| 2 inches | 162 sq ft | 0.62 cu yd |
| 4 inches | 81 sq ft | 1.23 cu yd |
| 6 inches | 54 sq ft | 1.85 cu yd |
| 8 inches | 40 sq ft | 2.50 cu yd |
| 12 inches | 27 sq ft | 3.70 cu yd |

## Board Feet Calculations

Board feet is the standard lumber measurement in North America:

```
Board Feet = (Thickness (in) × Width (in) × Length (ft)) ÷ 12
```

### Common Lumber Sizes in Board Feet

| Lumber Size | Board Feet per Linear Foot |
|-------------|--------------------------|
| 1×4 | 0.333 |
| 1×6 | 0.500 |
| 2×4 | 0.667 |
| 2×6 | 1.000 |
| 2×8 | 1.333 |
| 2×10 | 1.667 |
| 4×4 | 1.333 |
| 6×6 | 3.000 |

**Example:** 10 pieces of 2×4 at 12 ft each:
1. Board feet per piece = (2 × 4 × 12) ÷ 12 = 8 bd ft
2. Total = 10 × 8 = **80 board feet**

## Weight Conversions

### Pounds to Tons

Construction materials are often sold by the ton:

```
Tons = Pounds ÷ 2,000
```

| Material | Weight per cu yd (approx) |
|----------|--------------------------|
| Gravel | 2,800-3,000 lbs (1.4-1.5 tons) |
| Sand | 2,600-2,800 lbs (1.3-1.4 tons) |
| Topsoil | 2,000-2,500 lbs (1.0-1.25 tons) |
| Crushed stone | 2,800-3,200 lbs (1.4-1.6 tons) |

### Gallons Per Square Foot

For paint and coating projects:

```
Gallons Needed = Square Feet ÷ Coverage per Gallon
```

Most paint covers 350-400 sq ft per gallon. Multiply by number of coats:

**Example:** 500 sq ft room, 2 coats of paint:
1. Coverage per coat: 500 ÷ 350 = 1.43 gallons
2. Two coats: 1.43 × 2 = **2.86 gallons → 3 gallons**

## Linear to Area Conversions

### Paint Coverage
- **1 gallon of paint:** Covers ~350-400 sq ft
- **1 gallon of primer:** Covers ~300-350 sq ft
- **1 quart of paint:** Covers ~100 sq ft

### Flooring Coverage
- **1 box of tile:** Typically covers 10-15 sq ft
- **1 sheet of plywood:** 32 sq ft (4×8)
- **1 square of roofing:** 100 sq ft

## Size to Weight Conversions

Converting from material dimensions to weight requires the density:

| Material | Density (lb/ft³) | 1 cu ft weighs |
|----------|-----------------|---------------|
| Steel | 490 | 490 lbs |
| Concrete | 150 | 150 lbs |
| Water (reference) | 62.4 | 62.4 lbs |
| Dry sand | 100 | 100 lbs |
| Wet gravel | 125 | 125 lbs |

```
Total Weight = Volume (cu ft) × Density (lb/cu ft)
```

## Common Mistakes

### 1. Forgetting to Convert Inches to Feet
A 4-inch thick slab is 0.333 ft, not 4 ft. Always convert smaller units to match the formula.

### 2. Mixing US and Metric Units
Double-check that all dimensions use the same system. A project mixing feet and meters will be catastrophically wrong.

### 3. Rounding Too Early
Carry at least 2-3 decimal places through calculations. Round only the final number when ordering.

### 4. Ignoring Waste Factor
Always add 5-15% waste to every material estimate. No project uses 100% of ordered material.
