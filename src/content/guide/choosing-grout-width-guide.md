Grout is not just cosmetic; it fills the space between tiles, absorbs stresses from movement, and seals the subfloor from water infiltration. Selecting the right grout width is essential for a high-quality, durable tile installation.

## Standard Grout Line Widths

The width of your grout lines depends on the type of tile, sizing variation, and aesthetic preference:

* **1/16 Inch**: The minimum width recommended. Ideal for high-end rectified tiles (tiles cut to exact dimensions in the factory) and polished stone.
* **1/8 Inch**: The industry standard for ceramic and porcelain tiles. Provides a clean, modern look while absorbing minor size differences between tiles.
* **3/16 to 1/4 Inch**: Used for natural stone, handmade pavers, and quarry tiles, which have larger dimensional discrepancies.
* **3/8 Inch or Wider**: Used for rustic Saltillo tiles, brick pavers, or mosaic slate to match their irregular shapes.

---

## Sanded vs. Unsanded Grout Rules

The width of the grout line dictates the type of grout material you must buy:

| Grout Line Width | Recommended Grout Type | Why |
|------------------|-------------------------|-----|
| **Under 1/8 Inch** (e.g. 1/16") | **Unsanded Grout** | Sand particles are too large to fit in tight joints, causing voids. |
| **1/8 Inch and Wider** | **Sanded Grout** | The sand acts as an aggregate binder, preventing shrinkage and cracking as the grout cures. |

---

## Industry Guidelines (TCNA Rules)

The Tile Council of North America (TCNA) provides strict rules regarding grout spacing:

1. **Dimensional Tolerance**: A grout line cannot be narrower than the dimensional variation of the tile itself. If your tiles vary in width by 1/16 inch, your grout lines must be at least 1/8 inch wide to conceal the variation.
2. **Lippage Mitigation**: Large-format tiles (any tile with one side longer than 15 inches) are prone to bowing. To prevent lippage (uneven edges), use a grout joint of at least 1/8 inch and avoid running tiles in a 50% brick layout (offset by 33% maximum instead).
